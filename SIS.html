Liam Coll

Exploring the world of sound and graphics.

3/8/2020

Space Image and Sound started with a bang this semester, with my lecturer, Clint, setting us the task of writing a short proposal within the week. I instantly had the idea of using live radio data to produce some generative visuals etc. I went around researching and checking out the capabilities of Arduino radio components, but ultimately ran into issues as my computer doesn’t have a audio input. Also using Arduino would be pointless when I could just use an old radio. I kept prototyping and experimenting with Processing audio libraries, drawing waves according to the current audio volume. Here’s an example using the intro to “Tom's Diner” by DNA Feat. Suzanne Vega. (Bonus points if you know why I chose this song)

During the week I also managed to convert my old Guitar Hero guitar controller into a wireless MIDI input, albeit quite limited. I realised now that ultimately what I wanted to do this semester was take the opportunity to experiment, hack and tinker. My proposal to Clint was:

For Space Image and Sound this semester I want bring focus away from the ‘end product’ and more towards tinkering, experimenting and hacking, with the output to be a series of mini-projects that I can pull together in some way. This gives me the opportunity to experiment with unusual interfaces and recycling old technology to generate sounds and graphics, following my interest in analog audio as demonstrated by the excellent Look Mum No Computer (including his work ethic of high energy fast paced experimentation), as well as being low-cost and an enjoyable creative experience.



With this goal in mind, I began to tinker. I thought that a good starting point would be to follow the excellent Look Mum No Computer SIMPLEST OSCILLATOR DIY PROJECT. I had a decent attempt at making it, but ultimately wasn’t successful, although I had fun making it and breaking some old stuff I had lying around.

Over the weekend, I came back to the radio idea after a fellow student suggested a “lo-fi” approach to the sound input, i.e literally just pointing a microphone at a radio speaker. I started messing around with this and had a great time going crazy on the floor of my bedroom, experimenting with Ableton Live effects and crazy inputs.

I eventually ended up producing a soundscape with the radio, tuning up and down the channels and ‘playing’ the radio as if it were an instrument, a sort of live sampling. I edited the soundscape and paired it with some footage I took from around the house around the same time as the radio broadcast, creating a ‘snapshot of time’. Although this was overall quite quick to do and rather ‘lo-fi’ I ultimately want to have it all live, so no editing or videos or anything, and have generative art to go along with it (I’ve also been experimenting with MAX/MSP to achieve this). It’s a solid start to the paper however, and I’m really enjoying myself as doing stuff with audio is uncharted waters for me.


11/8/2020

Progress has been a bit slower over the past week, with my attention a bit more focused on other projects, but I spent today prototyping and generally messing around. Earlier on in the week I decided to take some time and step back to make sure I fully understood what I was working on. I hooked up the speaker that I had salvaged to an Arduino, and ran a PWM signal through it, which made a cool noise much to the dismay of my fellow studio space occupants. I decided to take it one step further and hooked up a MIDI keyboard to the speaker through Processing and the serial port. Notes played on the keyboard played through the speaker. After I did this, I decided I would try and get the oscillator from last week working, deeming it vital to my electronic sound generation experimentation. I couldn’t get it working using the technique I was following, but a fellow student recommended that I use the iconic 555 timer IC to get a pulsing signal. I hooked it up pretty quickly and made an LED flash as well as making the speaker make a buzzing sound. Very fun, very quick, very cheap, everything I wanted.


18/8/2020

Yikes. Literally an hour after I submitted the last post, we received an alert from the NZ Government that Auckland was heading into a level 3 lockdown. Yep, that pesky COVID-19 again. It’s really becoming a bit of a nuisance.
Unfortunately I left all my kit and tools etc. in studio and haven’t been able to organise a rescue mission since, so I haven’t made much headway on the electronical tinkering side of things.
However, I did have a short chat with Clint and Sam online, and we discussed what I could be doing effectively at this point, especially as it is currently unknown when we will be out of lockdown.
We also discussed some advantages of the humble radio, how it perseveres, how it will be one of the last parts of humanity to go in some apocalyptic event – morbidly appropriate in the current circumstances. I spent the afternoon taking some advice from them and produced a new radio soundscape. I’m a lot more happy with this one, I kept editing to a minimum and focused on effects, making the whole process more “live”. I also used some cool MIDI effects to generate a background ambient track.

Liam Coll · 18-8-20


2/10/2020

Not great at keeping this updated. My philosophy of “do first, write later” is potentially an issue.
Anyway, I didn’t get too much done during the rest of the first half-semester, focusing on other projects.
We had a formative submission due, where I outlined my plans and previous research. Clints feedback was positive, stating: “Really great work Liam! and so glad to see that you working on a project that you are clearly engaged with - keep up the momentum”.
I did as he advised. After a short mid-semester break I jumped back into it all. I now had access to studio, tools, my equipment and most importantly: Jaycar.
My initial task was to add a potentiometer to my previously built circuit, however I got quickly sidetracked by a new LOOK MUM NO COMPUTER upload, about a machine he built known as “The Atari Atari Atari Punk Punk Punk Console Console Console”. The machine is effectively the tripled version of a machine called the Atari Punk Console, which he recommended as an easy beginner project.
The machine uses a 556 dual timer chip, which is essentially two 555 chips strapped together. I was intrigued, and did some shopping at Jaycar, grabbing some potentiometers and the chip itself.
I got it working that night, with a bit of effort and started making some cool sounds. I was happy with this, and decided to replicate the circuit on some stripboard.


It didn’t work.
I don’t know why.
I still don’t.

It was at this point where I lost motivation. I started getting very frustrated with my inability to diagnose problems and design things for myself. After falling off the wagon, and with a deadline fast approaching, I decided to pull myself up and reconcile all the ideas I’d been working with over the semester.
I owned an old bedside alarm clock radio that was essentially junk. I had tried pulling it apart earlier on in the semester with no luck. I spent the evening using a bit more force, and gutted it completely.

I decided that the casing of the radio would be the enclosure for an Atari Punk Console, which I would reconstruct on a breadboard and place inside, and attach the potentiometers to the pre-existing knobs on the case.
This would be an elegant solution to bringing all my ideas together. I really liked the ideas from to conversation I talked about last post, about radios being the last thing to go during an apocalypse: they’re fundamental and persevere through anything, receiving and transmitting all throughout.
This idea lined up nicely with the raw noises created by the Atari Punk Console, simple and ever-produced, and all created using a basic circuit.
I set about planning the enclosure, deciding what I had to cut out, where to drill holes etc. I made a list so that when I went into the mech lab I would work more efficiently.

I also built the circuit again (took a lot longer than expected, pesky potentiometers).
I spent the next day in the mech lab, slowly ticking off all the things I needed to do. I had a really great time building the enclosure, ignoring circuitry for a little bit.
I’ve discovered working with my hands in this way is really rewarding, and a great opportunity to learn new skills with regards to using tools etc.

I finally put it all together and started to play with it (luckily studio was empty).

I still want to add a proper working switch (I hit the one I had with a hammer in what can only be called a poor decision), and work on getting the volume knob a bit easier to use, but its mostly done.
I decided to name the machine RadI/O, incorporating the inputs and outputs of the circuit, and the radio itself.


9/10/2020

I spent last Tuesday fixing up some stuff on RadI/O, making the knobs easier to use, and adding a knob to the volume potentiometer.



The machine now makes really consistent sounds and is really fun to play with. I showed it off to other students and TAs, who all seemed really excited about the noises they could make, with one even trying to sample a noise they produced for their own project.
I discussed my ideas and project intents with a TA, who was really interested, and recommended that I check out a video about circuit bending and its influence on the Dub musical genre.
I’m really satisfied with the way the project turned out. It’s been a bumpy ride with pandemics, motivation loss, dodgy circuitry and endless experimentation, but it all worked out in the end, with a bit of hard work and lots of creativity.
Giving myself room to experiment at the beginning was an excellent idea, as it took the pressure off what I MADE, and was more about what I DID.
Theres still a bit more that could be done. Could I somehow hook up the LCD screen to the audio signal? Create compositions with the noises? Attach the machine to a modular synth setup?
As always the project continues, but here it will end, for now. I have a really cool machine that I can pick up now and again, and push its boundaries sometime in the future.
